# Bonded-sphere discrete-element modelling of lithium orthosilicate pebble crushing for fusion blanket pebble beds

## Abstract

Ceramic breeder pebble beds in fusion blankets must retain mechanical integrity while accommodating thermal expansion, cyclic loading and purge-gas transport. Pebble crushing can alter both the load-bearing skeleton and the pore network, but experiments provide limited access to the internal breakage sequence of individual pebbles and their subsequent evolution inside a bed. Here we develop a bonded-sphere discrete-element workflow for lithium orthosilicate pebbles in which each 1.0 mm mother pebble is represented by 500 bonded subparticles in LIGGGHTS-INL. A weak-plane single-pebble calibration candidate reaches a peak load of 18.64 N and forms two major fragments, matching the current literature-scale load and morphology targets better than homogeneous templates. We then inject hundreds of these locked templates into gravity-settled random beds. Three independent 500-pebble beds show the same first breakage displacement, 0.0725 mm, and the same first damaged top pebble, while later damage depends strongly on upper-bed packing descriptors. A 1000-pebble short compression with targeted local bond dumps resolves six top-layer events between 0.0675 and 0.0950 mm, totaling 53 broken internal bonds in the highest mother pebble. These results demonstrate an end-to-end route from single-pebble calibration to mother-pebble-resolved breakage-event sequences in random packed beds. Final predictive use will require fuller experimental stiffness calibration, quasi-static verification, multi-seed 1000-pebble runs and native contact-force-chain output.

## Introduction

Lithium orthosilicate pebbles are candidate ceramic breeder materials for tritium breeding blankets, where packed pebble beds must transmit mechanical loads while preserving heat-transfer and purge-gas pathways. Mechanical degradation is therefore not only a strength problem of individual pebbles, but also a bed-scale transport and packing problem. Single-pebble crush tests provide direct measurements of contact strength, size effects and elastic response, whereas bed-compression and gas-flow experiments reveal collective consequences after rearrangement and crushing.

Discrete-element modelling offers a way to connect these scales, but common bed-scale treatments often represent crushing through empirical replacement rules, breakage probabilities or simplified particle failure criteria. Such approaches can reproduce selected macroscopic trends, yet they do not directly track the internal breakage network of an individual pebble or its transition into multiple fragments. A bonded-subparticle model provides a more explicit route: the pebble is treated as an assembly of smaller particles connected by breakable cohesive bonds, so that crack initiation, bond loss and fragment formation can be analysed from the same simulation.

Here we build this bonded-subparticle route for Li4SiO4 pebbles using LIGGGHTS-INL. We generate a 500-subparticle pebble, compress it between rigid mesh plates, record plate forces and intact bonds, and reconstruct fragments from the intact-bond graph. We then test whether bond strength, loading speed and template-level weak planes produce physically interpretable trends. After defining a current single-pebble calibration candidate, we inject the same template as an intact object into random packed beds formed by gravity settling of proxy 1 mm spheres. This separation of packing from internal breakage lets all internal bonds remain locked during bed formation, so that subsequent bond loss can be attributed to compression-driven crushing events.

## Results

### A bonded 500-subparticle Li4SiO4 pebble remains stable before loading

We first constructed a 1.0 mm nominal Li4SiO4 pebble from 500 subparticles. Initial overlapping subparticle templates produced non-physical early bond failure, so the final template used non-overlapping subparticles and an initial bond network containing 6723 bonds after LIGGGHTS-INL bond creation. In the smoke test, all 6723 bonds remained intact and no dangerous neighbour builds were reported. This established a stable initial state for subsequent compression.

### Mesh-plate compression produces simultaneous force, bond and fragment outputs

The bonded pebble was then compressed between two rigid mesh plates. The LIGGGHTS-INL cohesive bond model was enabled for both particle-particle and particle-wall contact, and a local contact-model whitelist was required for the bonded mesh-wall combination. Because `fix print` caused MPI aborts in this model combination, force and bond-count histories were extracted from the thermo table in `log.liggghts`. Intact-bond networks were dumped as local files and analysed as graphs, with subparticles as nodes and intact bonds as edges.

### Bond strength controls the crush-load scale and final bond loss

To test whether the model responds sensibly to bond strength, we compressed otherwise identical pebbles with tensile and shear bond strengths of 25, 50 and 100 MPa. Under the fast loading protocol, the peak top-plate force increased from 3.99 N at 25 MPa to 6.67 N at 50 MPa and 16.47 N at 100 MPa. Over the same displacement range, the final broken-bond count decreased from 242 to 193 and 123, respectively. The monotonic trend indicates that the bond-strength parameters are controlling the intended failure resistance rather than producing arbitrary numerical failure.

### Loading-rate sensitivity identifies a practical quasi-static regime for breakage count

The fast protocol is useful for screening but cannot be treated as quasi-static without checking loading-rate effects. We therefore repeated the 50 MPa case at 1.0, 0.5, 0.25 and 0.1 m s-1 while keeping the final displacement constant. The final broken-bond count decreased from 193 at 1.0 m s-1 to 123 at 0.5 m s-1, but then remained nearly unchanged at 105 and 106 for 0.25 and 0.1 m s-1, respectively. This indicates that the fragment count is still rate-sensitive at high loading speed but approaches a useful plateau at 0.25 m s-1 and below. Force oscillations remain visible and should be reduced using damping checks or a step-relax protocol before final calibration.

### Intact-bond graphs reveal fragment formation

For the 50 MPa, 0.1 m s-1 case, we dumped the intact bond network every 1000 steps and calculated connected components. The first fragmentation event occurred at step 218000, when a single subparticle detached from a 499-subparticle main fragment. At the final displacement, 6617 intact bonds remained and the pebble had separated into seven connected fragments. The largest fragment contained 494 subparticles, while the remaining six fragments were single subparticles. This early result suggests surface chipping rather than catastrophic fragmentation under the current parameter set.

### Bond stiffness constrains the usable calibration space

We next screened the normal bond stiffness at fixed 50 MPa bond strength, with the tangential bond stiffness set to half the normal value. The two lower stiffnesses, 5.0e13 and 1.0e14 N m-3, produced estimated initial stiffnesses of 452 and 570 N mm-1 and 53 final broken bonds over the screened displacement range. In contrast, 2.0e14 N m-3 produced an abrupt loss of 4701 bonds immediately after contact and was rejected from physical interpretation pending smaller-timestep verification. This screening step indicates that bond stiffness cannot be increased independently of timestep and loading protocol.

### A weak-plane template matches both crush-load and major-fragment targets

The homogeneous template could match the expected 1.0 mm crush-load scale but still failed by surface chipping. At 120 MPa and 0.1 m s-1, the peak top force was 18.45 N, but the final graph contained a 494-subparticle main fragment and six single-subparticle chips. Changing the global shear-to-tensile strength ratio did not remove this failure mode. We therefore introduced a two-type weak-plane template in which cross-plane bonds had a lower creation distance and lower strength than bonds inside each half of the pebble. The best current candidate, SP-002-CAL1, used bulk and cross-plane strengths of 90 and 22.5 MPa, respectively, with a cross-plane bond creation distance of 90 um. It reached a peak top force of 18.64 N and a peak bottom force of 18.07 N, while the final intact-bond graph contained two major fragments of 227 and 224 subparticles plus smaller chips. This case is the first single-pebble calibration candidate that satisfies both the literature-scale crush-load target and the requirement to avoid pure surface chipping.

To test whether this calibration depended on a single hidden weak-plane orientation, we repeated the same material parameters with four additional plane normals. All orientations retained two major fragments, but peak top force varied substantially: 18.64 N for the x-normal case, 27.37 N for a 30 degree vertical plane, 24.57 N for a 45 degree vertical plane, 28.38 N for the y-normal case and 14.52 N for a tilted x-z plane. The corresponding major fragments were 227/224, 223/220, 229/223, 228/216 and 237/234 subparticles. The five-case mean peak top force was 22.70 N with a standard deviation of 5.93 N. This pilot ensemble indicates that defect orientation can produce realistic strength scatter while preserving bulk splitting, and should therefore be treated as a stochastic microstructural variable in the final calibration.

Because orientation alone did not centre the ensemble on the literature target, we also tested a sample-level strength multiplier. For the high-strength y-normal orientation, the baseline 90/22.5 MPa bulk/weak strengths produced a peak top force of 28.38 N. Reducing both strengths by the same factor to 80/20 MPa lowered the peak to 20.60 N, while the final graph still contained two major fragments of 228 and 221 subparticles. Intermediate 74/18.5 MPa strengths gave 19.33 N and fragments of 228 and 222 subparticles, while a further reduction to 70/17.5 MPa gave 15.69 N and fragments of 227 and 219 subparticles. A linear-plan centre at 55.1/13.8 MPa underpredicted the target with a peak of 13.86 N. These runs show that a two-level calibration is feasible, but that the final ensemble should use orientation-specific response interpolation rather than assuming that peak load scales linearly with bond strength.

The calibration evidence is therefore organized in two levels. The experimental target table separates verified 0.5 mm crush-load anchors, digitized size-effect anchors, near-size qualitative anchors and neighbouring DEM literature (`tables/single_pebble_calibration_target_evidence_summary.csv`). The model ensemble table separates rejected homogeneous load-matched cases, the deterministic SP-002-CAL1 candidate, orientation-pilot scatter, strength-multiplier validation and early Weibull-conditioned trials (`tables/single_pebble_model_ensemble_evidence_summary.csv`). This distinction is important for the bed-scale results: the inserted template is a defensible current calibration candidate, not yet a final Li4SiO4 material law.

### A first bed-scale test exposes the importance of initialization

To test whether the same bonded template could be used beyond the single-pebble setting, we created a 12-pebble ordered bed with 6000 subparticles. The first bed spacing produced cross-pebble bonds during the initial bond-creation step, which is nonphysical. Increasing the centre spacing to 1.30 mm removed this artefact and produced exactly 80676 intact bonds, equal to 12 times the single-pebble bond count. In a short fast-loading test, the top plate reached 0.10 mm displacement, the peak top force reached 36.10 N, and 308 bonds broke. However, the bottom plate reaction remained zero, indicating that the current ordered bed had not yet formed a supported load-bearing structure. This result is therefore a workflow check rather than a bed-compression validation, and it identifies settling and lateral confinement as the next requirements.

A second bed-scale workflow, PB-002, used the same safe initial spacing and then attempted to densify the bed by top-plate compression. This preserved the intended internal bond count and did not create new cross-pebble cohesive bonds after the first timestep. However, even after 0.80 mm of top-plate displacement, the bottom reaction remained zero while 8419 bonds broke. The result confirms that bond creation can be controlled in multi-pebble simulations, but also shows that ordered, initially loose beds are not adequate for physically interpretable compression. A settling or random-packing stage is required before production bed simulations.

We therefore introduced a staged compact-bed initialization in PB-003. Twelve pebbles were first created at 1.35 mm centre spacing and run for one timestep to create only internal cohesive bonds. Each 500-subparticle pebble was then translated by atom-id group into a compact 2 x 2 x 3 bed. A 1.00 mm target-spacing diagnostic preserved the intended distinction between internal cohesive bonds and non-cohesive inter-pebble contacts, but relocation and preload broke about 140 internal bonds before the intended compression stage. Increasing the target spacing to 1.02 mm and placing the bottom plate at z = -1.506 mm removed this artefact: all 80676 internal bonds were preserved before compression, no new bonds were created after relocation, and both support and compression responses were obtained. In this supported baseline, the first compression-stage bond loss occurred at about 0.100 mm top displacement; at 0.12 mm displacement, the top force reached 26.15 N and 212 bonds had broken. Intact-bond graph analysis at the final sampled state gave 24 connected components, corresponding to the original 12 pebble-scale components plus 12 single-subparticle fragments.

Finally, we embedded the calibrated SP-002-CAL1 weak-plane template into a 12-pebble bed in PB-004. Because each calibrated pebble contains fewer cross-plane bonds than the homogeneous template, the initial bed contained 70512 internal bonds, or about 5876 per mother pebble. Per-pebble event tracking was performed by assigning atom-id ranges to mother-pebble ids and comparing each bond dump against the initial internal bond graph. In the supported 0.30 mm compression case, top-layer pebbles 9-12 broke first at 0.1025 mm top displacement. Middle-layer pebbles 5-8 began breaking later, at 0.2125 mm, while bottom-layer pebbles 1-4 remained intact through 0.30 mm. By the final displacement, the top layer had accumulated 658 broken internal bonds and the middle layer 160. This is the first end-to-end demonstration of calibrated 1 mm bonded templates producing a resolved bed-scale breakage-event sequence.

We then increased the bed to 36 mother pebbles in PB-005 using a 3 x 3 x 4 stack, giving 18000 subparticles and 211536 internal bonds in the deterministic orientation case. At 0.15 mm top displacement, the top layer had begun to break but the lower three layers remained intact. A fully ordered bed with identical weak-plane orientations broke artificially synchronously: all nine top-layer pebbles first lost bonds at 0.1287 mm and each accumulated 34 broken bonds by 0.15 mm. To remove this symmetry artefact, each 500-subparticle mother pebble was inserted with a random quaternion orientation so that the weak plane rotated with the whole template. In this random-orientation case, first breakage occurred earlier, at 0.1187 mm, and cumulative top-layer damage became heterogeneous: the nine top-layer pebbles accumulated 33-70 broken internal bonds by 0.15 mm, with 430 total broken bonds in the top layer. This larger-bed result shows that both sample number and microstructural orientation variability are required before bed-scale breakage statistics are meaningful.

### Random-packed 500-pebble beds produce reproducible onset but seed-dependent event growth

To move from ordered debugging beds to statistically meaningful beds, we then implemented PB-006 as a two-stage random-packing workflow. In the first stage, 1 mm proxy spheres representing whole mother pebbles were inserted from an upper factory region using `insert/rate/region` and settled under gravity. Because these proxy particles contain no internal bonded subparticles, the packing stage cannot cause internal pebble damage. In the second stage, the settled proxy centres were extracted and replaced by calibrated 500-subparticle SP-002-CAL1 bonded templates. The templates were first created far apart to form only their internal bond networks and were then relocated as intact units to the settled centres. In a 500-pebble initialization check, this procedure created 250000 subparticles and 2938000 intact internal bonds, exactly 500 times the 5876-bond calibrated template count, with zero broken internal bonds before compression. This establishes the locked-bond random-packing protocol required for subsequent compression-driven breakage-event statistics.

In the first PB-006 production compression, the 500-pebble random bed was compressed to 0.20 mm top displacement using primitive side and bottom walls and a moving mesh top plate. Up to the last local bond dump at 0.1975 mm, 271 internal bond breaks were localized to 11 mother-pebble events involving 5 mother pebbles. The first localized event occurred at 0.0725 mm in mother pebble 500, with 23 new broken bonds. Breakage remained strongly height-localized over this displacement window: the top height bin accumulated 223 localized broken bonds and the next bin 48, whereas lower height bins remained intact. The thermo endpoint at 0.20 mm reported 16 additional broken bonds after the last local dump, so subsequent runs include a final-step bond-local dump to assign terminal events to mother pebbles. This result is the first 500-pebble demonstration of a compression-driven, height-resolved breakage sequence using the calibrated bonded template.

A second independent 500-pebble random pack reproduced the same onset pattern while showing different event magnitudes. The seed02 pack settled to a height of about 4.08 mm and again initialized with 250000 subparticles, 2938000 intact internal bonds and zero broken bonds before compression. With the final-step local bond dump enabled, all terminal events could be assigned to mother pebbles. At 0.20 mm displacement, seed02 accumulated 246 localized broken bonds across 9 events and 4 mother pebbles. Its first event also occurred at 0.0725 mm in mother pebble 500 in the top height bin, but the first event contained 42 broken bonds rather than 23. The subsequent sequence contained a plateau with no new localized bond loss between 0.0975 and 0.1475 mm, followed by renewed breakage at 0.1725 mm and near the final displacement. Across both seeds, damage remained confined to the top two height bins over the 0.20 mm window, supporting a repeatable early sequence in which local load transfer near the moving plate activates a small number of upper pebbles before lower-bed breakage begins.

A third 500-pebble random pack confirmed the same first-break displacement and location but revealed stronger later-stage damage. Seed03 settled to a lower height of about 3.82 mm, suggesting a denser load-bearing arrangement than the first two packs. It again initialized with 2938000 intact internal bonds and zero broken bonds before compression. The first localized event occurred at 0.0725 mm in mother pebble 500, with 23 broken bonds, matching seed01. At 0.0975 mm, however, breakage spread laterally across the top layer: mother pebbles 498, 499 and 500 accumulated 41, 40 and 40 new broken bonds, respectively. By 0.20 mm, seed03 reached 559 localized broken bonds across 22 events and 7 mother pebbles, with a final top force of 47.86 N. Despite this stronger response, damage remained confined to the top two height bins, with 500 broken bonds in the top bin and 59 in the next bin. The three-seed set therefore separates a reproducible onset feature from seed-dependent event magnitude: all three packs first break at 0.0725 mm in the highest pebble, but denser or differently connected upper load paths can strongly amplify subsequent top-layer damage.

### Upper-bed packing and force-path proxies explain seed-dependent breakage magnitude

To quantify the packing origin of this seed dependence, we computed geometric contact-network descriptors from the settled mother-pebble centres. With a 1.02 mm centre-distance cutoff, seed03 had the lowest settled bed height, the largest top-bin population and the highest top-bin mean geometric degree. Specifically, the bed heights were 3.99, 4.08 and 3.82 mm for seed01, seed02 and seed03; the corresponding top-bin pebble counts were 15, 9 and 28; and the top-bin mean degrees were 3.00, 2.89 and 3.54. The highest mother pebble was also the first broken pebble in all three cases, but its geometric degree increased from 3 in seed01 and seed02 to 4 in seed03. The same qualitative ordering persisted when the contact cutoff was varied from 1.00 to 1.10 mm. These descriptors indicate that seed03 is not simply a stochastic outlier in bond failure; it begins compression with a more crowded and better connected upper load-bearing zone. The descriptor analysis does not yet replace direct contact-force-chain measurements, but it provides a physically interpretable bridge between random deposition geometry and the observed breakage-event cascade.

Finally, we tested whether the same locked-bond workflow can be scaled from 500 to 1000 mother pebbles. A 1000-pebble proxy bed settled to a height of 7.912 mm. Replacing these centres with 1000 SP-002-CAL1 bonded templates generated 500000 subparticles. A lightweight initialization check, designed to avoid writing multi-million-row local bond dumps, reported 5876000 created and intact internal bonds, exactly 1000 times the 5876-bond calibrated template count, with zero broken bonds. This confirms that the template-injection stage itself scales to 1000 pebbles.

We then ran a thermo-only short compression scan of this 1000-pebble bed to 0.10 mm top displacement. This scan used the same bonded contact model and locked-template initialization, but suppressed periodic particle and bond-local dumps to reduce I/O cost; a final bond-local dump was retained for endpoint localization. The first cumulative bond loss occurred at 0.0675 mm, slightly earlier than the 0.0725 mm onset in the three 500-pebble seeds. At the 0.10 mm endpoint, 53 internal bonds had broken, and comparison with the intact template graph assigned all 53 broken bonds to mother pebble 1000, the highest settled pebble. We therefore reran the same case with a targeted output window: no local bond dumps were written before 0.0575 mm, after which bond-local dumps were saved every 1000 steps through the 0.10 mm endpoint. This recovered six resolved mother-pebble events at 0.0675, 0.0700, 0.0775, 0.0900, 0.0925 and 0.0950 mm, with event sizes of 15, 8, 20, 6, 2 and 2 broken bonds. All six events occurred in mother pebble 1000 and all 53 broken bonds remained in the top height bin. Thus, the 1000-pebble result supports the same early top-layer trigger observed in the 500-pebble set, while remaining a short-displacement onset check rather than a full 0.20 mm event-statistics run.

## Discussion

The current results demonstrate a working computational chain for single-pebble breakage: bonded template generation, mesh-plate compression, force-displacement extraction, bond-breakage tracking and graph-based fragment analysis. This chain is essential for the proposed bed-scale study because it gives each inserted pebble an internal failure state rather than assigning breakage only at the whole-particle level.

Several limitations remain before the results can support final physical claims. First, the orientation and strength-multiplier scans are still pilot studies; a publishable Weibull comparison will require many more weak-plane orientations and stochastic strength multipliers. Second, the present loading speeds are numerical screening speeds; the 0.25 and 0.1 m s-1 cases suggest convergence in final fragment count, but force histories still require quasi-static verification. Third, high bond stiffness can trigger abrupt numerical failure unless the timestep and loading protocol are adjusted. Fourth, the current stiffness and failure-displacement comparison is still limited by incomplete extraction of experimental force-displacement curves. Finally, bed-level force-displacement data require carefully controlled initialization, support and confinement. The staged PB-003/PB-006 results narrow this problem: cross-pebble cohesive bonding can be avoided by creating internal bonds in a separated configuration and then translating intact groups into the compact bed, and calibrated 1 mm weak-plane templates can be tracked as mother-pebble breakage events. PB-006 further separates packing from breakage by using rigid proxy spheres for gravity deposition and introducing bonded templates only after the random bed geometry is known. The 1000-pebble targeted-window rerun shows that full-history local dumps are not required to recover the onset sequence: thermo scans can identify the onset window, and targeted local-dump reruns can then localize mother-pebble event increments. However, later 1000-pebble propagation is not yet established: deeper 0.15-0.20 mm attempts stalled or produced no compression-stage local bond data and are archived only as numerical diagnostics. Quantitative bed-scale predictions will still require multiple random packing seeds, stochastic template orientations and strength multipliers, restartable deeper 1000-pebble loading, and a loading protocol that maintains physically realistic force transfer through the bed.

The next stage is therefore to extract literature crush-load and elastic-slope data, calibrate bond stiffness and strength, and then inject calibrated bonded pebbles into a representative pebble bed. The bed simulations will test how local pebble crushing modifies macroscopic compression response, bond-breakage accumulation, fragment generation and packing evolution.

## Methods

### Bonded pebble generation

A nominal 1.0 mm Li4SiO4 pebble was represented by 500 spherical subparticles. A non-overlapping template was generated and exported to the LIGGGHTS-INL `particletemplate/multiplespheres` format. Bonds were created using the LIGGGHTS-INL cohesive bond model with a creation distance selected to produce a connected internal network. For the weak-plane calibration branch, subparticles were assigned to two material types according to their position relative to a vertical plane, and the LIGGGHTS-INL `spheres_different_types` option was used so that cross-plane bonds could be assigned a separate strength and creation distance.

### LIGGGHTS-INL implementation

Simulations used a local no-VTK build of LIGGGHTS-INL 4.0.0. The contact model was:

```lammps
pair_style gran model hertz tangential history cohesion bond stressBreak on createBondAlways off
```

Rigid compression plates were imported using `mesh/surface/stress`, and the upper plate was moved with `move/mesh`. Bond status was recorded using `compute bond/counter`. Intact bond pairs were written using `compute pair/gran/local/bond` and `dump local`.

### Post-processing

Thermo logs were parsed into CSV files using `scripts/extract_liggghts_thermo.py`. Force-displacement and bond-breakage summaries were generated by `scripts/analyze_sp002_curve.py`. Fragment counts were calculated by `scripts/analyze_bond_fragments.py`, which treats each intact bond as an edge in an undirected graph and reports connected components.

For PB-006 random-bed simulations, internal bond dumps were compared against the initial bonded template graph to assign each broken bond to a mother-pebble id and event timestep. Settled mother-pebble centres were used to compute geometric packing descriptors, including bed height, height-bin population and centre-distance-based geometric degree. Saved particle dumps were further analysed to reconstruct overlap-based inter-pebble contact networks. For these proxy force-path diagnostics, each cross-mother subparticle overlap contributed a weight proportional to `overlap^(3/2)`, and top-wall loading was estimated from subparticle overlap with the moving top plane. This is a geometry-derived Hertz proxy rather than a native LIGGGHTS contact-local force output.
